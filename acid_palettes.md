
# Acid Palettes

Acid palettes are high‑chroma, synthetic colour systems used to create ecstatic, toxic, candy‑radiant, chemically alive intensity. These palettes must remain governed by optical structure or they collapse into rainbow slop. Acid means bright with menace, not random.

## Core Identity
Acid palettes combine artificial sweetness, chemical brightness, unstable complements, toy‑like and toxic overlaps, blacklight adjacency, and ecstatic visual aggression. They are especially good for candy‑op worlds, glam imagery, contaminated surfaces, symbolic object ecologies, and surreal environments with synthetic atmosphere.

## Core Acid Palette Families
**1. Candy Acid** – Hot pink, orange, yellow, turquoise, purple. Feels playful, ecstatic, delicious, loud, and toy‑biome friendly.

**2. Toxic Acid** – Lime, magenta, cyan, acid yellow, with black/white anchors. Feels dangerous, chemically bright, unstable, and aggressive.

**3. Blacklight Acid** – Hot pink, cyan, violet, electric blue, with white highlights. Feels editorial, nocturnal, synthetic, glamorous, and ritual‑nightclub adjacent.

**4. Melted Sorbet Acid** – Coral, bubblegum pink, lemon, mint, lilac. Feels softer, sweeter, plush/candy oriented, and cute/eerie when contrasted with severe structure.

**5. Burnt Neon Acid** – Orange‑red, toxic green, magenta, ultraviolet purple, with black anchors. Feels dangerous, hot, feverish, and chemically overdriven.

## Structural Uses
Acid palettes intensify symbolic props, create toy‑biome atmospheres, make contamination feel radiant, stage ecstatic editorial images, sharpen route destinations, energise black‑and‑white scaffolds, and push plush worlds into a cute/eerie contradiction.

## Best Practices
- Preserve a monochrome or high‑value scaffold underneath.
- Choose one dominant acid hue family rather than all of them.
- Use saturation in clusters rather than uniformly.
- Anchor acid colours with black, white, or neutral darks.
- Decide whether the mood should be sweet, toxic, or ecstatic and curate hues accordingly.
- Keep symbolic objects and threshold zones chromatically important.

## Common Failure Modes
- **Rainbow Vomit:** Too many colours with equal intensity, turning the image into random noise.
- **Sweetness Without Teeth:** Cute colour without contrast or tension; the image loses threat.
- **Saturation Blanket:** Everything is saturated, leaving no rest zones and killing hierarchy.
- **Palette Confusion:** Warm and cool acids fight without purpose, creating muddy results.

## Anti-Drift Rules
- Use acid colour only where structure exists.
- Preserve force zones: centre, lips, edges, object highlights.
- Anchor with black, white, or strong value contrast.
- Let one hue family dominate; do not confuse loud with alive.
- Contrast sweetness with danger; add dark anchors and structural tension.

## Prompt Fragments
- “acid candy palette clustered in symbolic object zones over a black‑and‑white scaffold”
- “toxic synthetic palette using lime, magenta, and cyan with strong hierarchy”
- “blacklight acid colour concentrated at route markers and portal lips”
- “melted sorbet acid palette used on plush species but anchored by black”
- “burnt neon acid palette used in a feverish glam chamber with high contrast”

## Summary
Acid palettes are the high‑voltage drug cabinet of Retinal Surrealism. When controlled, they create ecstatic worlds that are simultaneously sweet and dangerous. When uncontrolled, they turn into rainbow mush. Control the acid; don’t let it control you.
